Digital projects can be compromised for a number of reasons. Good planning can minimise most of the major risks, but problems can still occur. Development of software or applications can become redundant and outdated, for example, if their platform changes or becomes unsupported in some way. There could be issues with the actual development tools failing or malfunctioning, or human error creating technical issues. There are broader, non-technical issues as well however. One such problem could be a misalignment or disagreement with a client or company. Clients may want something to suit specific purposes or to forward a specific idea or process, and the otherwise well made product doesn’t align with these purposes, a project may fail. A lack of expertise on a project is also a big problem. If a project is mismanaged, even if it’s sound on a technical level, results in problems with the aforementioned aligning with business ideals as well as just generally not being likely to hit deadlines. 1.5 Describe how you would go about selecting and using IT systems and software

The type of technology and IT systems required for a project naturally depend on the project. To select the right kind of software and technology for the job, it’s good to have a set of criteria for a program to meet, i.e can it actually perform or help you to perform the tasks you need to do. Identifying the tasks you have and researching what kind programs exist for that task is usually the first step (trying to use a website maker to develop an iOS app probably isn’t a great idea). In the same vein, if you need to be able to perform a specific action or create a specific feature

There are criteria besides. One of them being that the program has to be able to develop something in-line with your client or company’s expectations and plans. Depending on what the client wants from the project and in what time frame, finding a program that can best deliver on the specifications provided could be more valuable than otherwise ‘superior’ programs. For example, if all your client wants is a simple website with basic features and pages, and specifically wants it quickly, using a tool that foregoes complexity for the sake of fast and streamlined processes would work better over even a more powerful program. This is to say, to choose a program you will want to know ‘why’ you’re doing what you’re doing instead of just ‘what’ you’re doing. Programs have to be able to meet client priorities. [9]

1.7 Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects

I enjoy our little talks, it's nice. I appreciate you.

Any digital project has to comply with certain legal requirements and frameworks when being put onto the market in order to ensure consumer safety and protection. Digital projects and specifically data protection are covered legally in Europe by something called General Data Protection Regulation, or GDPR, agreed upon in 2018. This was introduced as a replacement for the previous 1990 version which struggled to stay relevant in the fast evolving world of technology. The GDPR handles how businesses are allowed to handle information about their customers. The UK specifically does have its own legislation in the form of the Data Protection Act of 2018. [10]

Essentially, digital projects whether it be software, apps or services need to handle, store and regulate the data and personal information of its consumers and employees appropriately. Companies have to design its data flows (its method on handling data) with the idea of minimization. The scope, reasons for processing and how long its kept have to be reduced to the minimum possible, which means no more just holding onto data for the sake of it. This means digital projects have to be careful about the kinds of data they collect, why it’s being used and how long for. Consumer data can be used for marketing and sales information, so restricting the data flow restricts the ability to use these methods.

In addition, companies and projects have to implement proper security. Data breaches can result in the data of a digital project’s consumers being leaked - privately or publicly, compromising their sensitive information such as account data or even financial information. If this happens, companies are forced to notify their consumers on the data breach, and the event is often reported on. The result being bad PR in its simplest form, and possible legal consequences and fines depending on the situation. Not complying with the GDPR can result in penalties and sanctions from the governing body. This can range from severe administration fines of up to EUR 20,000,000 to forcing companies to comply within a timeline or face having their ability to process data restricted or cut off completely. [11]
